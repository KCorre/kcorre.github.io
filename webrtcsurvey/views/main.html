Security on the Web is usually perceived by users through a green lock next to the URL adress bar of their browser. This icon informs users that the Website they are visiting is accessed through a secure connection. In addition, it also indicates that the web server has been authenticated through a cryptographic certificate. Thus the browser asserts that no attacker can break the confidentiality of the exchange and that no attacker is impersonating the website.

With the advent of technology such as WebRTC, real-time communications are moving to the Internet. The security of such communication is more complex than for a single web page as it now involves more actors. We propose a represenation of the trust that the end-user (you!) should have in a given online communication setup.

This survey will be used to evaluate the interest, usefulness, and clarity of our model as a security knowledge representation.

The first half of the survey is dedicated to your actual trust in real time communication services. The second half of the survey let you play with our model, before some questions on your perception of the way our model represents information.

The survey should not take more than 10 minutes to complete.

Trust in Audio-Video communications

In this section, we are interested in the trust you may have in different communication services or communication scenarios.

Real Time Communication services allow for usages such as voice call, video call, and file sharing. The communication is generally established by the exchange of signalling messages between participants. These signalling messages are relayed by one or more Communication Services (CS). CS are also responsible for authenticating their own participants when they connect to the service.

WebRTC is a standard API provided by Web browsers for RTC communications. As it is provided by web-browser any websites could become its own CS and offer RTC functionnalities. WebRTC also offers a way for call-participants to exchange identity assertion provided by a third-party Identity Provider (IdP). This IdP is independant from the communication services and allows users to authenticate directly to other participants. The identity assertion is verified by the browser which then asserts that Bob is the participant on the other end of the call.

Model - WebRTC Communication Setup decomposition

Here we model the security and trust of a WebRTC session. The idea is to represent the trust that a user may have in the confidentiality of his communication. We consider a call-session involving two participants, Alice (you) and Bob. Each participants use a different communication service, CSA and CSB.

Bob also provides an identity assertion signed by and verified through IdPB, his Identity Provider. This identity assertion binds the fingerprint of the session, FnP, to Bob's identity ensuring that no one is able to impersonate Bob.



The blue panel allows to act on trust relations between actors of the call setup. You can edit the trust of Alice to other actors with she directly interacts, but also the trust relations between other actors invisible. For instance between Bob's CS and Bob's IdP.

Leafs of the model represent security primitives of the model and can be edited by a double-click on the black circle. They represent the security parameters of the communication setup, for instance the password strength used by Bob or the individual TLS connections between two actors.

Finally, a set of pre-selected configurations can be selected to simulate different scenarios.
